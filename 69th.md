PROTOCOL
I. PREAMBLE: A VALEDICTORY DIAMOND JUBILEE
"This is the day the Lord has made; we will rejoice and be glad in it" (Psalm 118:24).
It is with a heart overflowing with gratitude that I welcome you all to the 60th Anniversary of our historic Annual National Convention (Diamond Jubilee). For sixty years, the faithful have gathered annually in holy convocation to seek the face of God. We stand here today to celebrate six decades of consistent fellowship, spiritual renewal, and the preservation of our collective bond in Christ.
However, this gathering carries a profound personal significance for me. As I stand before you today, my heart is filled with both joy and nostalgia, for this marks my final Presidential Address to this esteemed Convention before I formally retire and bow out of office later this year.

II. IN MEMORIAM: HONOURING OUR FALLEN HEROES
Before we proceed to celebrate our milestones, we must pause to remember those who are no longer with us. In recent times, our hearts have bled over the tragic losses and brutal murders of some of our devoted members due to the security challenges in our land. We extend our deepest condolences to their families. Their blood, spilled in innocence, cries out, and their unwavering faith remains a seed that will continue to grow this Church. May their gentle souls rest in perfect peace.

III. A DECADE OF STEWARDSHIP
As we celebrate this Diamond Jubilee of our Convention, I am also reminded that it has been nearly a decade since God placed the mantle of leadership upon my humble shoulders. Standing before you today, I wish to place on record the modest strides we have made together. These achievements are not mine alone, but the fruit of our collective labour, the support of the General Church Council (GCC), and the grace of the Almighty.

IV. EDUCATIONAL ADVANCEMENT & THEOLOGICAL TRAINING
Recognizing that a well-taught church is a strong church and that education is the bedrock of development, we prioritized revitalizing our institutions.
• Repositioning & Upgrading: We have successfully repositioned the Aleyideino Memorial Bible Institute (AMBI) to meet modern academic standards. This involved a comprehensive review of our curriculum to ensure our ministers are not only spiritually sound but intellectually equipped to tackle contemporary challenges.
• Digital Integration (AMBI): To drive digital theological education, we established the AMBI Online Centre. This bold step has broken the barriers of distance, allowing ministers across the

We have moved HEKAN towards a more professional, accountable, and secure administrative structure.
* Digital Tools & Efficiency: To enhance operational efficiency, we procured 20 laptops and distributed them strategically to equip the AMBI Online Centre, as well as the offices of the President, the General Secretary, and the Accounts Department. This protects our heritage and ensures that anyone holding a HEKAN Card is a verified and authenticated member of our fold.
* Accounting Standards: We conducted a comprehensive review and upgrade of our accounting books and records to align with global best practices. This commitment to financial probity has built unprecedented trust among our congregation and our international funding partners.
* Digital Presence: We have developed a functional and interactive website, making HEKAN accessible to the global community. It serves as a digital pulpit, a rich resource centre, and a vital bridge connecting us seamlessly with our members in the diaspora and international partners.

**IX. THE STATE OF THE NATION & KADUNA STATE**
As a Church, we do not exist in a vacuum. We live within the larger society, and the nation's socioeconomic realities deeply affect our congregation. It is our prophetic duty to speak truth to power and offer counsel for the good of our land.

• **On Insecurity:** While we acknowledge the ongoing efforts of our security agencies, the reality remains that the insecurity plaguing our beloved country has severely impacted our members. Farmlands have been abandoned, communities displaced, and tragically, innocent lives have been lost. We call on the Federal Government to urgently overhaul the national security architecture. We need proactive, intelligence-driven operations to secure every inch of Nigerian territory so that our people can sleep with both eyes closed and return to their lawful livelihoods without fear.
• **On Governance in Kaduna State:** We commend the Kaduna State Government for its developmental strides, particularly in rural and urban road construction, improve security, as well as its deliberate efforts toward inclusive governance. These infrastructural improvements are commendable steps toward modernizing our state peaceful coexistence, and opening up the economy.
• **On Agriculture & Food Security:** However, we must state unequivocally that the alarming reality of food shortages and hunger easily overshadows infrastructural strides. Considering the widespread poverty ravaging the land, we urge the government to implement actionable reforms that prioritize human welfare and national food security. To avert a full-blown food crisis, the government must aggressively subsidize agricultural inputs and ensure that these subsidies reach genuine farmers at the grassroots level. We urgently call for the timely provision of affordable fertilizers, tractors, and herbicides to empower our agrarian communities. Economic policies must have a "human face" and must be designed to alleviate the suffering of the commoner, not compound it.

Introduction: A united Christian family is the foundation of a stable church and society. As believers, we
are reminded in Scripture that we are one in Christ (Galatians 3:28), called to live in harmony, love, and
mutual respect. When families reflect this unity, they become a powerful testimony of God's design for
peace and stability, influencing the church and the wider community.

Just as the body of Christ thrives in unity, so does the Christian family. Disunity within families often leads
to division in the church and instability in society. However, when families embrace the principles of
forgiveness, understanding, and selfless love, they create an environment where faith can flourish, and
relationships can strengthen.

In this teaching, we will explore how the unity of Christian families impacts the Church's mission and
society's wellbeing, emphasizing the biblical call to live as one in Christ.
The formation of the family is God's idea and not man's. The first people God created were male and
female, called "mankind" or "Adam". It was God's idea to institute family (marriage), and the Lord also
founded the Church. We must understand that families formed the Church and society. The family unit
was long established before the establishment of the Church.

A family was founded by God (Gen.2:21-24; 9:1); it is one of the first and oldest institutions; it is as old as
the Bible. A Christian family, first and foremost, is rooted in a monogamous setting: one Christian man
marrying one Christian woman, both of whom form the foundation of the family, with or without siblings.
An ideal (dace) Christian family is one founded on Christian principles/values (Ka'idodi). It is distinct
from other families worldwide. Children born or adopted are members of a Christian family. It is
conscious of unity/love in Jesus Christ (Eph. 5:21ff; Col. 3:18ff; Romans 8:15). Above all, "It is a copy of
the highest and most perfect union, that of the Church with Christ its head" (McClintock and Strong).

As such, the family unit is so important that it cannot be ignored when discussing the unity of the Church
and society. However, more importantly, when it comes to "We are one in Christ." Once the individual
family or family unit is unstable, it will negatively affect the existence of the Church. In other words, a
dysfunctional family would give birth to an unstable Church and society. What the Church, society, and
the world at large are today is the reflection of the individual or small units of the families that form the
Church and the society. There is no doubt the world is in a serious crisis, and these crises are the overflow
from the Church, which originates from individual families or family units." Therefore, if the family is
stable and united in Christ, both the Church and society will be stable."

Furthermore, the practical link between a united family and a stable society cannot be overstated. When a
family operates in unity, it serves as the first school of human relations. Children who are raised in homes
where parents model conflict resolution, active listening, and unconditional forgiveness naturally carry
these virtues into their communities. They become honest civil servants, fair business leaders, and
compassionate neighbours. In the Church, these same individuals are less likely to engage in destructive
politics or power struggles because they have already learned the value of putting others first within their

UNDERSTANDING NIGERIA'S 2025 TAX REFORMS: IMPLICATIONS FOR THE CHURCH, CLERGY, AND CONGREGATION

by

ABDULRAUF ALIYU, Ph.D

Being a paper with the theme focus "Institutional Compliance, Clergy Obligations, Informal Sector Inclusion, and Technology-Driven Tax Administration under the 2025 Nigerian Tax Laws" presented at the United Church of Christ in Nigeria's (UCCN) 60th National Convention held at the HEKAN Church Headquarters at 4-6 Katsina Road, by Independence Way, Kaduna, Nigeria

April, 2026

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This paper examines the implications of Nigeria's 2025 tax reforms - comprising the Nigeria Tax Act (NTA), Nigeria Tax Administration Act (NTAA), Nigeria Revenue Service (NRS) Act, and Joint Revenue Board (JRB) Reform Act) - for the Church and its wider community. It highlights a fundamental shift toward a compliance-driven, transparent, and technology-enabled tax system aimed at broadening the revenue base and promoting national development.

The paper clarifies the distinction between tax-exempt ecclesiastical income (such as tithes and offerings) and taxable commercial activities (including schools, clinics, and farms), emphasizing the need for proper financial segregation and record-keeping. It further outlines the obligations of clergy and church employees under the Pay-As-You-Earn (PAYE) system, including the relevance of the N800,000 tax-free threshold and Tax Identification Numbers (Tax-ID).

Attention is also given to the informal sector, where many church members operate, providing practical guidance for gradual compliance and formalization. Additionally, the paper underscores the growing role of banking systems and digital technology in tax administration, particularly in transaction tracking and audit processes.

Ultimately, it positions the Church as a critical agent in promoting tax awareness, ethical compliance, and responsible citizenship, thereby aligning spiritual stewardship with national economic transformation.

SECTION 1: GENERAL INTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUND

The Nigerian fiscal landscape is undergoing one of its most significant transformations in recent history. The enactment of the Nigeria Tax Act (NTA) 2025, the Nigeria Tax Administration Act (NTAA) 2025, the Nigeria Revenue Service (Establishment) Act (NRS) 2025, and the Joint Revenue Board (JRB) Reform Act 2025 represents a deliberate and coordinated effort by the government to reposition taxation as a central pillar of national development. These reforms are not merely legislative adjustments; they embody a philosophical shift toward accountability, transparency, efficiency, and inclusiveness in tax administration.

Historically, Nigeria's tax system has been characterized by fragmentation, low compliance, weak enforcement, and a heavy reliance on oil revenues. This structure created vulnerabilities, particularly during periods of economic instability and fluctuating global oil prices. Consequently, the need for a more sustainable and internally generated revenue base became imperative. The 2025 reforms are therefore designed to broaden the tax base, strengthen institutional capacity, and ensure that taxation contributes meaningfully to national growth and social equity.

At the core of these reforms lies a compliance-driven philosophy. The new framework emphasizes that
taxation is not merely a statutory obligation but a civic responsibility that supports collective development.
This is evident in provisions such as Section 12 of the NTA 2025, which underscores the obligation of all
taxable persons and entities to maintain accurate records and file returns in a timely manner. Similarly,
Section 18 of the NTAA 2025 introduces stricter penalties for non-compliance, including failure to remit
taxes deducted at source.

Another defining feature of the reforms is the integration of technology into tax administration. The NRS
2025 mandates the use of digital systems for data sharing between financial institutions and tax authorities.
This is particularly relevant in Section 25 of the NRS Act, which provides for automated financial reporting
systems aimed at detecting discrepancies and improving audit efficiency. The implication is clear: the era
of informal and undocumented financial activities is gradually being replaced by a system where
transparency is enforced through technology.

For faith-based organizations such as churches, these reforms carry unique implications. While religious
institutions have traditionally benefited from tax exemptions on ecclesiastical activities, the new laws draw
clearer boundaries between exempt and taxable activities. Offerings, tithes, and donations remain largely
exempt under Section 30 of the NTA 2025; however, commercial ventures operated by churches—such as
schools, hospitals, guest houses, and farms—are subject to taxation where they generate income.

This distinction is particularly important for the United Church of Christ in Nigeria (HEKAN), given its
extensive institutional presence and community engagement. The reforms require the Church not only to
comply with statutory obligations but also to educate its members on the implications of these changes.
This educational role aligns with the Church's broader mission of stewardship, integrity, and social
responsibility.

Furthermore, the reforms seek to harmonize tax administration across federal, state, and local levels. The
JRB Reform Act 2025 strengthens coordination among tax authorities, reducing instances of multiple
taxation and jurisdictional conflicts. Section 7 of the JRB Act establishes a unified framework for tax policy
implementation, ensuring consistency and fairness across the country.

In essence, the 2025 tax reforms represent a transition from a loosely regulated system to one defined by
structure, accountability, and enforceability. For the Church and its members, this transition presents both
challenges and opportunities. On one hand, there is a need to adapt to stricter compliance requirements; on
the other hand, there is an opportunity to foster a culture of financial discipline and lawful engagement with
the state.

This paper is therefore situated within this evolving context. It seeks to provide clarity, guidance, and
practical insights into how the Church, its leadership, and its congregation can navigate the new tax
environment effectively. By demystifying the provisions of the law and aligning them with real-life
scenarios, the paper aims to bridge the gap between legislation and everyday practice.

The Genesis and Establishment of the Church (1954–1965)
The United Church of Christ in Nigeria (UCCN), popularly known as HEKAN, originated in 1954 when Nigerian contingents of the Sudan United Mission (S.U.M.), predominantly soldiers, prison personnel, and police officers, arrived in Kaduna. Their initial joint worship began at the Mogadishu Barracks and later progressed to a jointly built church at Gwari Road. Doctrinal disagreements over infant baptism with the Sudan Interior Mission (S.I.M.) eventually led the S.U.M. members, under the leadership of the Founder, Late Rev. Habila Aleyideino, and others, to establish a separate identity. As recorded by Baba Orume, by 1958, the contingents had completely moved out of the SIM and SUM joint fellowship.

The Church officially started in 1961 when the name "United Church of Christ in Nigeria" (UCCN/HEKAN) was adopted. However, the exact formal establishment and registration of the Church with the Corporate Affairs Commission (CAC) was achieved in 1965. In that same year, the church was subsequently accepted into TEKAN.

The HEKAN Annual National Convention commenced in 1966. I bring you Calvary greetings and welcome you to this historic 60th Annual National Convention of our great denomination. It is a profound privilege to present the summary of the Church's journey, reflecting on our achievements, challenges, and aspirations as we continue to labour faithfully in the Lord's vineyard.

As we gather for this milestone Diamond Jubilee, we are also profoundly mindful that this year marks a season of historic transition. We use this unique platform to formally acknowledge the exemplary and visionary leadership of our President, Rev. (Dr.) Amos G. Kiri, who will be gloriously retiring from active service in November this year. His tenure has been marked by unprecedented infrastructural growth, spiritual reawakening, and a firm commitment to the constitutional integrity of HEKAN. We urge the entire Church to remain in steadfast prayer for a smooth, divinely orchestrated transition.

1. KEY ACHIEVEMENTS (NASARORI)
A. Membership Growth and Church Expansion: By the sustaining Grace of God, the Church has witnessed a remarkable increase in membership across the DCCs, leading to the establishment of new congregations. Specifically, we celebrate the opening of Prayer Houses in Ogere and Shuwari, Abeokuta under HEKAN DCC Ikeja. Additionally, 5 mission fields were successfully handed over to DCC Jalingo, totalling 318 active worshipers. We also successfully erected a temporary worship structure at Isheri, Lagos, and recorded the baptism of 54 individuals during the year under review.

B. Contributions of Church Groups and Fellowships: We deeply appreciate the vibrant and continuous contributions of our various Church Sub-groups, including the Youth Fellowship, ZME, the Choir, and others.

C. Community Development and Humanitarian Service: In the Church’s unwavering quest to provide humanitarian services and reflect the love of Christ, the leadership, through our esteemed partners, successfully provided select communities with essential social amenities such as schools, clinics, and boreholes. We commend the diligent work of the HEKAN Pension Board for their steadfastness in managing retirement benefits and ensuring that those who have faithfully laboured in the Lord’s vineyard are not forgotten in their twilight years.

2. CHALLENGES (KALUBALE)
Despite our notable victories, the Church navigated several daunting hurdles during the year:

A. Financial Constraints: The prevailing global financial crises, the generally low income among members, and the depreciation in the market value of food commodities severely impacted the Church’s financial capacity. Virtually all the DCCs faced economic challenges that limited their ability to fully support and execute vital planned projects, programmes, and administrative initiatives.

B. Insecurity: Insecurity remained a major and persistent challenge facing the HEKAN Church across most of our DCCs, particularly along the Kasan Hausa axis. These issues negatively affected the smooth running of Church activities, disrupted the communal unity of members, and hindered the Church’s overall growth. Violent attacks in some communities made it extremely difficult for members to gather freely for worship. Tragically, one such attack in Kauru, Kuya, and Nassarawa Rogo resulted in the painful loss of our pastor, Rev. Yahaya Kambasaya, and several other beloved members.

C. Shortage of Workforce: A critical spiritual and administrative challenge facing the majority of our DCCs today is the severe shortage of pastoral workforce. The harvest is indeed plentiful, but the labourers are few. The Church urgently requires dedicated individuals who have received a genuine divine calling to serve in the pastoral ministry. We use this medium to earnestly appeal to those whose hearts the Lord is stirring to serve God in His vineyard, not just any youth, but those who are truly called, to yield themselves to vocational ministry and work faithfully for the Lord.

3. FUTURE PLANS (HANGE)
Looking ahead with faith, the Church is firmly committed to pursuing the following strategic objectives:
A. Continuous Development of Infrastructure: Recognizing that robust infrastructural development is vital for future growth, the Church plans to improve and construct adequate physical facilities better to support worship, administration, and community service as our membership expands. In line with this vision, the Church will focus intensely on completing ongoing legacy projects, including the HEKAN Secretariat, AMBI, Clinics, and Schools.
B. Capacity Building through Seminars and Workshops: To strengthen the spiritual, social, and leadership capacities of our members, the Church will prioritize organizing targeted seminars and workshops. We plan to hold these interactive consultations at the zonal level to gather direct feedback and constructive input from members on Church leadership and spiritual growth.
C. Institutional Accountability and Discipline: To protect the integrity of the Church, the General Secretariat, working in tandem with the Executive Council, is intensifying efforts to enforce strict financial accountability and constitutional discipline across all DCCs and LCCs. The Church will not compromise on transparency, and we mandate all leadership levels to adhere strictly to the approved financial guidelines of HEKAN.
D. Leadership Succession: The Church has officially embarked on the crucial and sensitive task of developing a comprehensive leadership succession plan to ensure lasting stability, institutional progress, and seamless continuity within our leadership ranks for the generations to come.
E. Digital Evangelism: Adapting to our rapidly advancing technological world, the Church plans to fully embrace digital tools as a strategic method for fulfilling the Great Commission. Digital evangelism will provide us with the unprecedented opportunity to reach a wider, global audience beyond our physical Church walls, engage active youths and adults online, and provide continuous spiritual nourishment through easily accessible, faith-building content.
May the Lord continue to bless, protect, and abundantly expand the United Church of Christ in Nigeria (HEKAN).
